# How it works

## Repeated squaring in a group of unknown order

The computation at the heart of a VDF is almost embarrassingly simple:

$$
y = x^{2^T} \bmod N
$$

computed by literally squaring $T$ times in a row, each squaring depending on
the previous result:

$$
y_0 = x, \qquad y_{i+1} = y_i^2 \bmod N, \qquad y = y_T
$$

There's no known way to compute $x^{2^T} \bmod N$ faster than $T$ sequential
squarings — not with more parallel hardware, not with a shortcut — **as long
as nobody knows the order of the group** $N$ defines. That last condition is
load-bearing: if you *did* know the group's order $\varphi(N)$, you could
reduce the giant exponent $2^T$ modulo that order first and then compute the
result with a short, parallelizable modular-exponentiation instead of $T$
sequential steps.

Two ways to build a group of unknown order are used in practice:

- **An RSA modulus** $N = p \cdot q$, for two large primes $p, q$. Nobody
  should know $p$ and $q$ individually — whoever generated them must destroy
  that factorization, because knowing it means knowing the group's order and
  breaking sequentiality. This requires a **trusted setup**: someone has to
  be trusted to have actually forgotten $p$ and $q$.
- **A class group of an imaginary quadratic field.** These don't require a
  trusted setup at all — the group's order is genuinely unknown to everyone,
  by construction, from public parameters — at the cost of more complex
  underlying math. Chia Network is the production system built on this
  approach — a future Applications page on this site will cover it in
  depth.

## The Wesolowski proof

Simply publishing $y$ isn't enough — anyone could claim any $y$. The prover
needs to also produce a short proof that $y$ really is $x^{2^T} \bmod N$,
checkable without redoing the $T$ squarings. Benjamin Wesolowski's
construction — a future History page on this site covers the citation and
the alternative Pietrzak construction — does this with one extra group
element:

$$
\ell = \text{hash\_to\_prime}(x, y, T)
$$

a prime derived by hashing the statement itself — hash $(x, y, T)$ to a
number, then search upward (checking primality with a Miller–Rabin test)
until landing on a prime. Both prover and verifier compute $\ell$ the same
way, so nothing needs to be transmitted for it.

The prover then computes:

$$
q = \left\lfloor \frac{2^T}{\ell} \right\rfloor, \qquad r = 2^T \bmod \ell, \qquad \pi = x^q \bmod N
$$

Computing $r$ is a single fast modular exponentiation of the small base 2 —
**not** $T$ sequential steps, since $r$ only needs $2^T \bmod \ell$, and
$\ell$ is small enough that this is a standard fast modpow. $\pi$ is the
actual proof, sent to the verifier alongside $y$.

## Verification

The verifier recomputes $\ell$ the same way the prover did, recomputes $r$
the same way, and checks one equation:

$$
\ell' = \text{hash\_to\_prime}(x, y, T), \qquad r' = 2^T \bmod \ell'
$$

$$
y \stackrel{?}{=} \pi^{\ell'} \cdot x^{r'} \pmod N
$$

This holds because $2^T = q\ell + r$ by construction, so $x^{2^T} = (x^q)^\ell
\cdot x^r = \pi^\ell \cdot x^r \pmod N$ — exactly the right-hand side above.
**Verifying takes a small, fixed number of fast modular exponentiations, no
matter how large $T$ was.** That asymmetry — genuinely sequential to compute,
cheap to check — is the entire point of the construction.

## Two things this page won't let you gloss over

**Trusted setup, honestly stated.** An RSA-modulus-based VDF is only as
sequential as the guarantee that nobody knows $N$'s factorization. Generating
$p$ and $q$ yourself and using them in your own computation is **not** a
trusted setup in any meaningful sense — it's the opposite: you'd know the
group's order and could skip straight to the answer, defeating the entire
purpose. This site's interactive demo, coming in the next phase, will
generate its own RSA modulus client-side purely for demonstration purposes,
and will say so plainly rather than imply that's how a real deployment
works. A real deployment needs either an actual multi-party trusted-setup
ceremony on a cryptographically-sized modulus, or a class group, which needs
no trusted setup at all — Chia Network does the latter, covered on a future
Applications page.

**Proof generation is simplified here, honestly stated.** Computing $\pi =
x^q \bmod N$ by a direct modular exponentiation costs roughly the same number
of sequential-ish operations as computing $y$ itself did, since $q$ is
almost as large as $2^T$. Production systems use smarter techniques —
caching intermediate powers computed along the way during Eval, and splitting
Wesolowski's proof into many phases computed in parallel ("$n$-Wesolowski",
used by Chia) — to make proof generation meaningfully faster than
recomputing the delay from scratch. The code and interactive demo on this
site (coming in later phases) use the direct, un-optimized computation of
$\pi$: correct, and fast enough at demo-sized $T$, but not a demonstration
of those production speedups.
